Like the universe itself, the cosmology and astrophysics program at Brown is expanding. Cosmology is the study of the origin, evolution and fate of the universe. Astrophysics, on the other hand, seeks to understand the physics of objects in the universe, such as black holes, stars and galaxies. The exact division between cosmology and astrophysics is nebulous and each relies on the other. In cosmology, graduate students are engaged in research to understand the physics of the very earliest universe and the evolution to its current state. In astrophysics, graduate students are developing a detector to measure the solar neutrino flux and spectrum. Robert Brandenberger's theoretical cosmology group explores the tantalizing connections between fundamental physics and cosmological observations. Many observations such as the observed anisotropy of the cosmic microwave background (CMB), the baryon-to-photon ratio, the spectrum of ultra-high energy cosmic rays, and the origin of dark matter, can only be explained using physics which occurred in the very early universe. The energy densities at which this physics took place are much higher than can be reached in any terrestrial particle accelerator. The best testing ground for new theories of fundamental physics, such as string theory, is the early universe.

The theoretical cosmology group focuses on investigations of theories for the origin of structure in the Universe, and on developing specific predictions for CMB anisotropy and gravitational lensing maps. Other focal points are studies of the origin of the matter/antimatter asymmetry, and searches for observational signatures of new theories of fundamental physics, such as string theory.

Ian Dell'Antonio studies the distribution of matter in the universe using the technique of gravitational lensing. Light from distant galaxies is bent when it passes by a foreground mass distribution. 

By studying the distorted shapes of these distant galaxies, the distribution of matter in the foreground mass is inferred. The clustering properties of matter on various scales can be used to study both the nature of the dark matter and the geometry of the universe. By studying the clustering of matter on relatively small scales, such as individual galaxies or the centers of clusters of galaxies, we seek to differentiate between different types of candidate dark matter particles. By studying the distribution of matter on the largest scales, measuring how the clustering evolves with redshift, and especially comparing the clustering with the pattern of distortions found in the CMB, we can determine what sort of universe we live in. In addition, we are also using other tracers, such as the distribution and velocities of galaxies and X-ray luminous clusters of galaxies in the distant universe, to study the evolution of structure.

Measuring the cosmic microwave background (CMB), relict light formed 300,000 years after the Big Bang, provides precision determination of the physics and events that occurred in the early universe. Measurements of the CMB even provides clues to the state of the universe 10-35 seconds after the Big Bang, a regime not accessible by direct observation. Greg Tucker's group is making observations of the CMB from the ground and is involved in the MAP satellite project. By looking at the very first galaxies to form in the universe, which appear brightest at submillimeter wavelengths, we will obtain a better understanding of how galaxies and structures of galaxies formed. The group is developing a balloon-borne instrument called BLAST to make these observations. Thesis students typically design and build a special purpose instrument and then use the instrument to make observations.

Students can expect to learn a wide range of experimental techniques that overlap with other disciplines represented in the department such as condensed matter and high energy particle experiment.

The particle astrophysics effort of Robert Lanou, Humphrey Maris, and George Seidel focuses on high rate measurements of neutrinos produced in the Sun. They are developing a new technique in the HERON experiment which uses superfluid helium as the target. Helium at superfluid temperatures (~50 mK) provides a completely radioactivity-free and compact target. This technique will distinguish the flux and spectra of neutrinos produced in p-p and Be7 reactions in the Sun. Unlike other current detectors for low energy neutrinos, the superfluid helium detector is sensitive to all neutrino types, not just electron neutrinos.

In addition, this group is developing low temperature calorimeters that are providing the sensitivity necessary to make a number of experiments in astrophysics possible. They are focussing on the development of devices that use the measurement of the magnetization of paramagnetic ions to detect the absorption of single x-rays with very high energy resolution.
